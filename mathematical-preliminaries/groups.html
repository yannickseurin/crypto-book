Basic Definitions

A group is a non-empty set G equipped with a binary operation ⋆ satisfying the following properties:

  • associativity: for every a,b,c∈G, (a⋆b)⋆c=a⋆(b⋆c);
  • identity element: there exists e∈G such that for every a∈G, e⋆a=a⋆e=a; e is called the identity element of G;
  • inverse element: for every a∈G, there exists b∈G such that a⋆b=b⋆a=e; b is called the inverse of a.

Proposition. Let G be a group. Then G has a unique identity element and every element a∈G has a unique inverse.

Proof. Assume that G has two identity elements e and e. Then e⋆e′=e (because e′ is an identity element) and e⋆e′=e′ (because e is an identity element), hence e=e′. Assume that some group element a has two inverses b and b′. Then b​=b⋆e=b⋆(a⋆b′)=(b⋆a)⋆b′=e⋆b′=b′.​

The group consisting of a single element e such that e⋆e=e is called the trivial group.

If the binary operation is commutative, i.e., for every a,b∈G, a⋆b=b⋆a, then G is said to be abelian.

If G is finite, the number of elements of G is called the order of G and denoted ∣G∣. If G is infinite, G is said to have infinite order.

Additive/Multiplicative Notation

There are two standard notation types for the group operation:

  • additive: the group operation is denoted +, the identity element is denoted 0, and the inverse of a is denoted −a; moreover, for k∈Z and a∈G, one defines ka:=⎩⎨⎧​0k termsa+⋯+a​​−((−k)a)​if k=0if k>0if k<0;​
  • multiplicative: the group operation is denoted ∗ or ⋅, the identity element is denoted 1, and the inverse of a is denote a−1; moreover, for k∈Z and a∈G, one defines ak:=⎩⎨⎧​1k termsa∗⋯∗a​​(a−k)−1​if k=0if k>0if k<0.​

In the case of multiplicative notation, the operation symbol might be omitted and the group law simply denoted by juxtaposition. By convention, for an "abstract" group, additive notation is restricted to abelian groups (meaning multiplicative notation is used either when the group is known to be non-abelian, or when the abelian/non-abelian character of the group is unspecified).

For the rest of this chapter, unless specified otherwise, the group operation will be denoted multiplicatively but the identity element will be denoted e for clarity.

Direct Product

Let (G,⋆) and (H,∙) be two groups. The direct product of G and H is the Cartesian product G×H:={(a,b)∣a∈G,b∈H} equipped with the binary operation ⋄ defined component-wise: (a,b)⋄(c,d)=(a⋆c,b∙d).

Proposition. The direct product as defined above is a group. Its identity element is (eG​,eH​), where eG​ and eH​ are respectively the identity element of G and H. The inverse of (a,b)∈G×H is (a−1,b−1).

For abelian groups, the direct product is sometimes called direct sum and denoted G⊕H.1

Subgroups

Let G be a group and H be a non-empty subset of G. The subset H is a subgroup of G if H equipped with the binary operation of G is a group.

The following proposition gives a handy subgroup criterion.

Proposition. Let G be a group and H be subset of G. Then H is a subgroup of G if and only if e∈H and for every a,b∈H, ab−1∈H.

A subgroup of G is said to be proper if it is different from G. Any non-trivial group has at least one proper subgroup, namely {e}, called the trivial subgroup.

Proposition. The intersection of a (finite or infinite) set of subgroups is a subgroup.

Proof. Let H=∩i∈I​Hi​ be the intersection of a collection of subgroups Hi​, i∈I. Then, by the previous proposition, e∈Hi​ for every i∈I and hence e∈H. Let a,b∈H. Then, for every i∈I, a,b∈Hi​ and hence ab−1∈Hi​ again by the previous proposition. Thus, ab−1∈H. It follows that H is a subgroup.

Cosets and Lagrange Theorem

Let H be a subgroup of G and g∈G. The left coset , resp. right coset of H associated with g is the subset of G defined as gHHg​:={gh∣h∈H}:={hg∣h∈ H}.​

Lagrange's Theorem. Let G be a finite group. Then the order of any subgroup H of G divides the order of G.

Proof. Consider the relation defined by a∼b if and only if ab−1∈H. One can easily check that this is an equivalence relation and that the equivalence class of g∈G is the right coset of H associated with g. For any g∈G, the mapping h↦hg is a bijection from H to Hg: it is obviously surjective and hg=h′g⇒hgg−1=h′gg−1⇒h=h′, hence it is injective. Hence, all right cosets have ∣H∣ elements. Since right cosets form a partition of G, we have ∣G∣=n∣H∣ where n is the number of right cosets.

The number of right cosets of H (which is equal to the number of left cosets) is called the index of H in G and denoted [G:H]. Hence, Lagrange theorem states that ∣G∣=[G:H]∣H∣.

Normal Subgroups and Quotient Groups

Let G be a group. A subgroup H of G is said to be normal if for every g∈G, gH=Hg (i.e., left and right cosets are equal).

Normality can be characterized by a number of other equivalent conditions. The easiest to check is often the following one.

Proposition. A subgroup H of G is normal if and only if for every g∈G, gHg−1⊆H.

For abelian groups, the situation is pretty simple.

Proposition. Every subgroup of an abelian group is normal.

Proof. If G is abelian and H is a subgroup of G, then for any h∈H, ghg−1=gg−1h=h and hence gHg−1=H. By the previous proposition, this implies that H is normal.

Let G be a group and let ∼ be an equivalence relation on G. We say that ∼ is compatible with the group structure of G if a∼b and c∼d implies ac∼bd. If ∼ is compatible with the group structure, then one can equip the quotient set G/∼ (the set of all equivalence classes) with a binary operation defined as [a][b]=[ab], where [a] denotes the equivalence class of a∈G. This is well defined as compatibility of ∼ with the group structure ensures that this binary operation does not depend on the specific representatives a and b of each equivalence class. Moreover, one can easily check that G/∼ equipped with this binary operation is a group with identity element [e] and where [a]−1=[a−1].

Proposition. Let G be a group and let ∼ be an equivalence relation on G. Then ∼ is compatible with the group structure of G if and only if there exists a normal subgroup H such that a∼b⇔ab−1∈H.

Proof. Assume that there exists a normal subgroup H such that a∼b⇔ab−1∈H. Let us show that ∼ is compatible with the group structure of G. Let a,b,c,d∈G such that a∼b and c∼d. Then ac(bd)−1=acd−1b−1=acd−1a−1ab−1. We have cd−1∈H because c∼d, which implies that acd−1a−1∈H because H is normal. We also have ab−1∈H because a∼b, hence ac(bd)−1=acd−1a−1ab−1∈H and ac∼bd.

Conversely, assume that ∼ is compatible with the group structure of G. Define H as [e], the class of the identity element. Let us first show that H is a normal subgroup. Let a,b∈H, i.e., a∼e and b∼e. Then, by compatibility of ∼ with the group structure, we have a∼e​⇒ab−1∼eb−1⇒ab−1e∼b−1b⇒ab−1∼e.​​(b−1∼b−1)(e∼b)​ Hence ab−1∈H and H is a subgroup.

To show that H is normal, let us show that for every g∈G, gHg−1⊆H. Let g∈G and h∈H. Then h∼e​⇒gh∼g⇒ghg−1∼gg−1⇒ghg−1∼e.​​(g∼g)(g−1∼g−1)​ Hence ghg−1∈H and H is normal.

It remains to show a∼b⇔ab−1∈H. By compatibility of ∼ with the group structure, we have a∼b⇒ab−1∼bb−1⇒ab−1∼e and ab−1∼e⇒ab−1b∼b⇒a∼b. Hence a∼b⇔ab−1∼e⇔ab−1∈H, which concludes the proof.

Hence, normal subgroups completely characterize the equivalence relations ∼ for which the quotient set G/∼ can be naturally equipped with a group structure.

Let H be a normal subgroup of G and let ∼ be the equivalence relation defined by a∼b⇔ab−1∈H. Then the quotient set G/∼ equipped with the binary operation defined by [a][b]=[ab] is called the quotient group associated with H and denoted G/H. Note that the order of G/H is [G:H], the index of H.

Homomorphisms and Isomorphisms

Let G and G′ be two groups. A group homomorphism is a function f from G to G′ such that for every a,b∈G, f(ab)=f(a)f(b). If f is bijective, then f is called a group isomorphism and groups G and G′ are said isomorphic, denoted G≅G′. If moreover G=G′, f is called a group automorphism.

Proposition. Let G and G′ be two groups and f:G→G′ be a group homomorphism. Then:

  • f(eG​)=eG′​,
  • for every a∈G, f(a−1)=f(a)−1,
  • for every subgroup H of G, f(H):={f(a)∣a∈H} is a subgroup of G′,
  • for every subgroup H′ of G′, f−1(H′):={a∈G∣f(a)∈H′} is a subgroup of G.

Let f:G→G′ be a group homomorphism. The kernel of f is the subset of G defined as ker(f):={a∈G∣f(a)=eG′​}. The image of f is the subset of G′ defined as im(f):={f(a)∣a∈G}.

By the previous proposition, ker(f) is a subgroup of G since it is equal to f−1({eG′​}) and im(f) is a subgroup of G′ since it is equal to f(G).

First Isomorphism Theorem. Let f:G→G′ be a group homomorphism. Then ker(f) is a normal subgroup of G and G/ker(f)≅im(f).

Proof. Let us first show that ker(f) is normal. Let g∈G and h∈ker(f). Then f(ghg−1)​=f(g)f(h)f(g−1)=f(g)eG′​f(g)−1=f(g)f(g)−1=eG′​.​ Hence gker(f)g−1⊆ker(f) and hence ker(f) is normal.

Consider now the mapping fˉ​:G/ker(f)→im(f) defined by fˉ​([a])=f(a). It is well-defined since [a]=[b]​⟺ab−1∈ker(f)⟺f(ab−1)=eG′​⟺f(a)=f(b).​ In other words, equivalence classes of G/ker(f) are just subsets of elements of G with the same image under f. Consequently, the definition of fˉ​ does not depend on the representative of the equivalence class.

It is a group homomorphism since fˉ​([a][b])​=fˉ​([ab])=f(ab)=f(a)f(b)=fˉ​([a])fˉ​([b]).​ Moreover, it is injective since fˉ​([a])=fˉ​([b])⇔f(a)=f(b)⇔[a]=[b]. It is also surjective since any element in h∈im(f) is of the form f(a) for some a∈G and hence h=fˉ​([a]). Hence, fˉ​ is a group isomorphism.

Group Generation

Let G be a group and A be a subset of G. The subgroup generated by A, denoted ⟨A⟩, is the intersection of all subgroups of G containing A. (Recall that by Proposition, an intersection of subgroups is a subgroup.) Informally, it is the "smallest" (for inclusion) subgroup of G which contains A: any subgroup containing A contains ⟨A⟩.

The following proposition gives a more explicit characterization.

Proposition. Let G be a group and A be a subset of G. Then ⟨A⟩ is the subgroup of all elements of G that can be expressed as the finite product of elements of A and inverse of elements of A.

Note in particular that ⟨∅⟩={e} (in which case the previous proposition still holds with the convention that an empty product is equal to e).

If A={a1​,…,ak​} is finite, the subgroup generated by A is also denoted ⟨a1​,…,ak​⟩. When G is abelian, then ⟨a1​,…,ak​⟩={a1z1​​⋯akzk​​∣z1​,…,zk​∈Z}.

A group G is said to be finitely generated is there exists a finite number of elements g1​,…gk​∈G such that G=⟨g1​,…,gk​⟩, in which case {g1​,…,gk​} is called a generating set of G.

A group G is said cyclic (or monogenous2) if there exists g∈G such that G=⟨g⟩, in which case g is called a generator of G.

The order of an element a∈G is the order of the subgroup ⟨a⟩. If G has infinite order, the order of an element a∈G can be finite or infinite.

Below we list a number of properties of the order of an element.

Proposition. Let G be a group and a∈G be a group element. Then a has finite order if and only if there exists k∈N∗ such that ak=e. In that case, a's order is the smallest integer n≥1 such that an=e and one has ⟨a⟩={e,a,a2,…,an−1}.

Proposition. If G has finite order n, then the order of any element a∈G divides n. In particular, for any a∈G, an=e.

Proof. The first part is a direct consequence of Lagrange's theorem. For the second part, let m be the order of a and write n=dm. Then an=adm=(am)d=ed=e.

Proposition. Let G be a group and a∈G be an element of order n. Then for every k∈Z, ak=e if and only if n divides k.

Proof. If n divides k then k=dn for some integer d, which implies ak=adn=(an)d=ed=e. Conversely, assume that ak=e. By Euclid's division lemma, there exists q,r∈Z such that k=qn+r and 0≤r<n. Then ak=aqn+r=(an)qar=ar and consequently ar=e. This implies that r=0 as otherwise a would have order r<n. Hence, k=qn and n divides k.

Proposition. Let G be a group, a∈G be an element of order n, and k∈Z. Then the order of gk is n/gcd(n,k).

Proof. Let d=gcd(k,n) and let ℓ be the order of gk. Then (gk)ℓ=gkℓ=e and hence by Proposition, n∣kℓ, which implies (dn​)∣(dk​)ℓ. Since gcd(dn​,dk​)=1, this implies dn​∣ℓ.

On the other hand, (gk)dn​=(gn)dk​=edk​=e, and hence by Proposition, ℓ∣dn​. We conclude that ℓ=n/d.

Properties of Cyclic Groups

Proposition. Any cyclic group is abelian.

Proof. Let G be a cyclic group, let g be a generator of G, and let a,b∈G be two group elements. Then there exists k,ℓ∈Z such that a=gk and b=gℓ, which implies that ab=gkgℓ=gk+ℓ=gℓgk=ba.

Proposition. Let G be a cyclic group and H be a subgroup of G. Then H and G/H are cyclic.

Proof. Let G be a cyclic group, g be a generator of G, and H be a subgroup of G. Let us first show that H is cyclic. If H={e} then H is clearly cyclic. Otherwise, let n≥1 be the smallest integer such that gn∈H (which necessarily exists since H contains at least one element different from e and either this element or its inverse can be written gm for some m≥1). We will prove that H=⟨gn⟩. Clearly, ⟨gn⟩⊆H since H is a subgroup. Conversely, let a∈H. Then a=gk for some k∈Z. By Euclid's division lemma, there exists q,r∈Z such that k=qn+r and 0≤r<n. Then a=gk=gqn+r=(gn)qgr, and consequently gr∈H. This implies that r=0 as otherwise this would contradict the minimality of n. Hence a=(gn)q∈⟨gn⟩ and H⊆⟨gn⟩. Thus, H=⟨gn⟩ and hence H is cyclic.

Let us now show that G/H is cyclic. More precisely, let us prove that G/H=⟨[g]⟩. Let [a]∈G/H be an element of the quotient group specified by an arbitrary representative a∈G. Then there exists k∈Z such that a=gk. Thus, [a]=[gk]=[g]k and hence [g] is a generator of G/H.

Proposition. Any group with prime order is cyclic and any element different from the identity element is a generator of G.

Proof. Let G be a group of prime order p. Let a∈G be an element different from the identity element and let n be the order of a. Since the order of an element divides the order of the group by Proposition, one has either n=1 or n=p. Since a=e, one cannot have n=1, hence n=p and a generates G.

Proposition. Let G be a cyclic group of order n , g be a generator of G, and k∈Z. Then ⟨gk⟩=G if and only if gcd(n,k)=1. In particular, G has ϕ(n) generators, where ϕ is Euler's function.

Proof. We have ⟨gk⟩=G if and only if the order of gk is n, which by Proposition is equivalent to gcd(n,k)=1.

For the second part of the proposition, write G={e,g,…,gn−1}. Then generators of G are exactly elements of the form gk with gcd(n,k)=1 and hence there are ϕ(n) such elements.

Proposition. Let G1​ and G2​ be two cyclic groups of order n1​ and n2​ respectively. Then the direct product G1​×G2​ is cyclic if and only if gcd(n1​,n2​)=1. Moreover, (g1​,g2​) is a generator of G1​×G2​ if and only if g1​ is a generator of G1​ and g2​ is a generator of G2​.

Proof. Let us first show the following lemma: Let a1​∈G1​ be an element of order k1​ and a2​∈G2​ be an element of order k2​. Then (a1​,a2​) has order lcm(k1​,k2​).

Let e1​ be the identity element of G1​ and e2​ be the identity element of e2​. Then (a1​,a2​)k=(e1​,e2​)​⟺a1k​=e1​∧a2k​=e2​⟺k1​∣k∧k2​∣k.​ The smallest such positive integer k is by definition lcm(k1​,k2​), which proves the lemma.

Let us now prove the proposition. Assume that G1​×G2​ is cyclic and let (g1​,g2​) be a generator of G1​×G2​. Then clearly g1​ must be a generator of G1​ and g2​ a generator of G2​. By the previous lemma, (g1​,g2​) has order lcm(n1​,n2​). On the other hand, (g1​,g2​) has order ∣G1​×G2​∣=n1​n2​ as it generates G1​×G2​. Hence, lcm(n1​,n2​)=n1​n2​, which implies gcd(n1​,n2​)=1.

Conversely, assume that gcd(n1​,n2​)=1 and let g1​ be a generator of G1​ and g2​ be a generator of G2​. By the lemma, (g1​,g2​) has order lcm(n1​,n2​)=n1​n2​=∣G1​×G2​∣ and hence generates G1​×G2​.

Classification of Cyclic Groups

The set of integers Z={…,−2,−1,0,1,2,…} equipped with addition is a cyclic group of infinite order with identity element 0 and generators 1 and −1. For any integer n∈N, the set nZ:={nz∣z∈Z} is a subgroup of Z of infinite order. It is cyclic and generated by n and −n. These are in fact the only subgroups of Z.

Proposition. Let G be a subgroup of Z. Then there exists a unique n∈N such that G=nZ.

Proof. Le us show existence first. If G={0} then G=0Z. Assume now that G={0}, implying that G contains at least one positive integer (it contains at least one non-zero integer a and either a or −a is positive). Let n be the smallest positive integer in G. Let us show that G=nZ. Clearly, nZ⊆G since n∈G. Conversely, let k∈G. By Euclid's division lemma, there exists q,r∈Z such that k=qn+r and 0≤r<n. Since n and k are in G, r=k−qn is also in G, which implies r=0 as otherwise this would contradict the minimality of n. Thus, k=qn∈nZ and G⊆nZ. Hence, G=nZ which proves existence.

For uniqueness, assume that G=nZ=n′Z for n,n′∈N. Then n∈n′Z implies n′∣n and n′∈nZ implies n∣n′, and consequently n=n′ since n and n′ are in N.

For any n∈N, we can consider the quotient group Z/nZ. The equivalence class of a∈Z is [a]={a+nz∣z∈Z}, also called the residue class of a modulo n. There are n distinct classes. Hence, the index of nZ is n and Z/nZ has order n. It is cyclic with generator [1] (and [−1]). More generally, by Proposition, the generators of Z/nZ are [k] for k∈Z such that gcd(k,n)=1.

Another way to think of Z/nZ is as the set {0,1,…,n−1} equipped with "modulo n" addition and inverses. More precisely, let Zn​ be the set of symbols {[[0]],[[1]],…,[[n−1]]} equipped with the binary operation [[a]]+[[b]]=[[(a+b)modn]]. This is a group with identity [[0]] and inverse −[[a]]=[[(−a)modn]]. One can easily see that Z/nZ and Zn​ are isomorphic, the isomorphism being [a]↦[[amodn]]. From now on, we will work with the latter formalism.

For any integer m∈Z, mZn​:={m[[u]]∣[[u]]∈Zn​} is a subgroup of Zn​. What does this subgroup look like? One has [[a]]∈mZn​​⇔∃[[u]]∈Zn​,[[a]]=m[[u]]⇔∃u∈{0,1,…,n−1},a=mumodn⇔∃u∈Z,a=mumodn⇔∃u,v∈Z,a=um+vn⇔gcd(m,n)∣a​(by Beˊzout’s lemma).​ Hence, letting d=gcd(m,n), we see that mZn​ is the subset of Zn​ containing multiples of d, i.e., mZn​={[[kd]]∣k=0,…,n/d−1}=dZn​. In particular, mZn​ has order n/d and index d. (As we will see shorty, mZn​ being cyclic, one has mZn​≅Zn/d​.)

Again, we can show that these are in fact the only subgroups of Zn​.

Proposition. Let n∈N and G be a subgroup of Zn​. Then there is a unique d∈N such that d∣n and G=dZn​.

Proof. Let G be a subgroup of Zn​. Let us prove existence first. Let H:={a+kn∣[[a]]∈G,k∈Z}. Then H is a subgroup of Z: it is clearly non-empty and for b,b′∈H, b=a+kn and b′=a′+k′n, one has b−b′​=(a−a′)+(k−k′)n=(a−a′modn)+k′′n​for some k′′∈Z,​ hence b−b′∈H. Thus, there exists d∈N such that H=dZ. Moreover, since 0∈G, n∈H and hence d∣n. Since G={[[a]]∣a∈H∩{0,…,n−1}}, it follows that G=dZn​.

Let us now prove uniqueness. Assume that G=dZn​=d′Zn​ for d,d′∈N with d and d′ dividing n. Since [[d]]∈dZn​, this implies [[d]]∈d′Zn​={[[kd′]]∣k=0,…,n/d′−1} and hence d′∣d. Conversely, d∣d′ and hence d=d′ since d and d′ are in N.

We can now prove the following two "structure theorems" stating that, up to isomorphism, Z and Zn​ are the only cyclic groups of infinite, resp. finite order n.

Fundamental Theorem of Cyclic Groups, Infinite Order. Let G be a cyclic group of infinite order. Then G is isomorphic to Z and the subgroups of G are exactly the subsets Gn:={an∣a∈G} for n∈N.

Fundamental Theorem of Cyclic Groups, Finite Order. Let G be a cyclic group of order n. Then G is isomorphic to Zn​ and the subgroups of G are exactly the subsets Gd:={ad∣a∈G} for d∈N such that d∣n. In particular, G has exactly one subgroup of order d for each divisor d of n, namely Gn/d.

Proof. Let G be a cyclic group and g be a generator of G. Consider the mapping f:Z→G defined by f(k)=gk. Then f is a group homomorphism since f(k+ℓ)=gk+ℓ=gkgℓ=f(k)f(ℓ). It is clearly surjective since g is a generator of G.

Consider first the case where G has infinite order. Let us show that f is injective. Assume that f(k)=f(ℓ) for distinct integers k and ℓ with k<ℓ. Then f(ℓ−k)=gℓ−k=e, contradicting the fact that g has infinite order. Hence, f is an isomorphism form Z to G. Since the subgroups of Z are exactly nZ for n∈N, the subgroups of G are exactly f(nZ)​={gk∣k∈nZ}={gkn∣k∈Z}={an∣a∈G},​ where the last equality follows from g being a generator and hence G={gk∣k∈Z}.

Consider now the case where G has finite order n. Let us show that ker(f)=nZ. Since g has order n, by Proposition, for every k∈Z, gk=e⇔k∣n, which exactly means that k∈ker(f)⇔k∈nZ. Hence, ker(f)=nZ. By the first isomorphism theorem, im(f)=G≅Z/nZ≅Zn​.

Let fˉ​:Zn​→G be the isomorphism defined by fˉ​([[k]])=gk. Since the subgroups of Zn​ are exactly dZn​ for d∈N such that d∣n, the subgroups of G are exactly fˉ​(dZn​)​={gk∣[[k]]∈dZn​}={gkd∣k=0,…,n−1}={ad∣a∈G}.​

Cauchy's Theorem for Abelian Groups

Lagrange's theorem states that the order of a subgroup of a finite group divides the order of the group. Conversely, given a divisor d of the order of the group, does there always exist a subgroup of order d? A group where this property holds is called a converse Lagrange theorem (CLT) group. The answer is no in general. However, there are specific cases where the existence of a subgroup is guaranteed. Cauchy's theorem states that for every group G (non-necessarily abelian) of finite order and every prime divisor p of ∣G∣, G has a subgroup of order p. Cauchy's theorem is a special case of Sylow's (first) theorem.

Here, we will only prove it in the easier case where G is abelian. Actually, a more general result (that will follow easily from the fundamental theorem of finite abelian groups) is that any finite abelian group is CLT. In other words, for a finite abelian group of order n, the existence of a subgroup of order d is guaranteed for any divisor d of n, not only for prime divisors.

Cauchy's Theorem (Abelian Case). Let G be an abelian group of finite order n. Then, for every prime divisor p of n, there exists a subgroup of G of order p (or, equivalently, there exists an element of G of order p).

Proof. The equivalence between the two conclusions follows from the fact that a group of prime order is necessarily cyclic.

Let {a1​,…,ar​} be a generating set of G and for i∈{1,…,r} let ni​ denote the order of ai​. Consider the mapping f:⟨a1​⟩×⋯×⟨ar​⟩→G defined by f(x1​,…,xr​)=x1​⋯xr​. Since G is abelian, it is a group homomorphism. Moreover, since {a1​,…,ar​} is a generating set, f is surjective (indeed, by definition of a generating set of an abelian group, any element y∈G can be written as y=a1k1​​⋯arkr​​ for some integers k1​,…,kr​∈Z and for each i∈{1,…,r}, aiki​​∈⟨ai​⟩). By the first isomorphism theorem, G is isomorphic to (⟨a1​⟩×⋯×⟨ar​⟩)/ker(f), which implies that ∣G∣⋅∣ker(f)∣​=∣⟨a1​⟩×⋯×⟨ar​⟩∣=n1​⋯nr​,​ and hence n=∣G∣ divides n1​⋯nr​.

Let p be a prime divisor of n. Then p divides n1​⋯nr​ and hence p divides ni​ for some i∈{1,…,r}. Then we can write ni​=kp and by Proposition, aik​ has order ni​/gcd(ni​,k)=ni​/k=p, which concludes the proof.

Exponent of a Group

Let G be a group. Consider the subset of Z defined as {k∈Z∣∀a∈G,ak=e}. One can easily check that this is a subgroup of Z. Hence, by Proposition, there is a unique integer m∈N such that this subgroup is equal to mZ. This integer m is called the exponent of G. Equivalently, it is defined as the smallest positive integer m≥1 such that ∀g∈G, gm=e. If no such integer exists, depending on the convention, G is said to have exponent 0 or infinite exponent. A finite group of order n necessarily has finite exponent m satisfying m∣n (since by Proposition, an=e for every a∈G and hence n∈mZ). Moreover, the order of any group element divides m by Proposition. Conversely, a group with infinite exponent necessarily has infinite order. However, a group with finite exponent is not necessarily finite.

In the following, we prove that an abelian group or finite exponent m always contains a element of order m. This will be a key lemma for proving the fundamental theorem of finite abelian groups. Note that none of the three following propositions holds for a non-abelian group.

Proposition. Let G be an abelian group and a1​ and a2​ be two elements of respective order n1​ and n2​ such that gcd(n1​,n2​)=1. Then the order of a1​a2​ is n1​n2​. More generally, if a1​,…,ar​ are r group elements of respective orders n1​,…,nr​ such that gcd(n1​,…,nr​)=1, then the order of a1​⋯ar​ is n1​⋯nr​.

Proof. Let n be the order of a1​a2​. Since (a1​a2​)n1​n2​=(a1n1​​)n2​(a2n2​​)n1​=e, by Proposition, we have n∣n1​n2​.

On the other hand, since (a1​a2​)n=e, one has a1n​=a2−n​ and hence a1n​∈⟨a2​⟩. This implies that (a1n​)n2​=a1nn2​​=e, hence n1​∣nn2​. But since gcd(n1​,n2​)=1, n1​∣n. Symmetrically, one also has n2​∣n. Since gcd(n1​,n2​)=1, this implies n1​n2​∣n and hence n=n1​n2​. The generalization can be proved by induction on r.

Proposition. Let G be an abelian group and a1​ and a2​ be two elements of respective order n1​ and n2​. Then there exists an element of G of order lcm(n1​,n2​).

Proof. Let p1e1​​⋯prer​​ be the prime factor decomposition of lcm(n1​,n2​). For each i∈{1,…,r}, piri​​ divides either n1​ or n2​. Say it divides n1​ (the reasoning is similar if it divides n2​). Then, by Proposition, a1n1​/piri​​​ has order n1​/gcd(n1​,n1​/piri​​)=piri​​. Hence, for each i∈{1,…,r}, there exists an element bi​ of order piri​​. By Proposition, b1​⋯br​ has order p1e1​​⋯prer​​=lcm(n1​,n2​).

Proposition. Let G be an abelian group of finite exponent m. Then there exists an element of G of order m.

Proof. By Proposition, the order of any group element divides m. In particular, all group elements have order at most m and hence there exists a group element of maximal order m′≤m. Assume towards a contradiction that m′<m. If the order of every group element divides m′, then for every a∈G, am′=e, contradicting the minimality of m. Otherwise, assume that there is a group element of order m′′ which does not divide m′. Then, by Proposition, there exists an element of order lcm(m′,m′′)>m′ contradicting the maximality of m′. Hence, it must be that m=m′, which concludes the proof.

As a direct corollary, we have that a finite abelian group is cyclic if and only if its order is equal to its exponent.

Structure Theorem for Finite Abelian Groups

This section presents the fundamental theorem of finite abelian groups, sometimes called Kronecker theorem. As we will see, finite abelian groups can be "decomposed" in two ways. The equivalence between these two decompositions relies on the following theorem.

Chinese Remainder Theorem for Groups. Let n1​ and n2​ be two positive integers. Then Zn1​​×Zn2​​≅Zn1​n2​​ if and only if gcd(n1​,n2​)=1.

Proof. Assume that gcd(n1​,n2​)=1. Consider the mapping f:Z→Zn1​​×Zn2​​ defined by f(z)=(zmodn1​,zmodn2​). One can easily check that f is a group homomorphism. Moreover, z∈ker(f)⇔(n1​∣z)∧(n2​∣z)⇔n1​n2​∣z, where the last equivalence follows from gcd(n1​,n2​)=1. Hence, ker(f)=n1​n2​Z. By the first isomorphism theorem, Zn1​n2​​≅Z/n1​n2​Z is isomorphic to im(f). In particular, ∣im(f)∣=∣Zn1​n2​​∣=n1​n2​=∣Zn1​​×Zn2​​∣, hence im(f)=Zn1​​×Zn2​​. Thus, Zn1​n2​​≅Zn1​​×Zn2​​.

Conversely, assume that gcd(n1​,n2​)=d>1. By Proposition, Zn1​​×Zn2​​ is not cyclic. As Zn1​n2​​ is cyclic, these two groups cannot be isomorphic.

For an integer n, one may ask how many different abelian groups of order n there are, up to isomorphism. Structure Theorem for Finitely Generated Abelian Groups

We now consider abelian groups which are finitely generated (but not necessarily of finite order).

Let G be an abelian group. For n∈N∗, an element a∈G is said to be an n-torsion element if an=e (or equivalently, if a has finite order k dividing n). An element a∈G is said to be a torsion element if it has finite order.

Proposition. Let G be an abelian group. Then the set of all n-torsion elements of G, denoted G[n], is a subgroup called the n-torsion subgroup of G and the set of all torsion elements of G, denoted GT​, is a subgroup called the torsion subgroup of G.

Proof. Since e is clearly an n-torsion element, G[n] is non-empty. Let a and b be two n-torsion elements. Then (ab−1)n=an(bn)−1=ee−1=e, hence ab−1 is an n-torsion element. Hence G[n] is a subgroup of G.

Similarly, e is a torsion element, hence GT​ is non-empty. Let a and b be two torsion elements of order respectively k and ℓ. Then (ab−1)kℓ=akℓ(bℓk)−1=eℓ(ek)−1=e. Hence ab−1 has finite order, i.e., it is a torsion element. Hence GT​ is a subgroup of G.

If G=GT​ then G is called a torsion group (or periodic group). If G={0} then G is said torsion-free.

Fundamental Theorem of Finitely Generated Abelian Groups. Let G be a finitely generated abelian group. Let GT​ be the torsion subgroup of G. Then GT​ is finite and abelian and there exists a free abelian subgroup F such that G=F×GT​. In particular, there exists a integer r∈N (called the free rank or simply rank of G) and integers n1​,…,nℓ​ with n1​∣⋯∣nℓ​ such that G≅Zr×Zn1​​×⋯×Znℓ​​. Integers r and n1​,…,nℓ​ are unique.
