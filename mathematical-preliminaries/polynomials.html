Generalities

Let F be a field (not necessarily finite). A (univariate) polynomial with coefficients in F is an infinite sequence (ai​)i∈N​ such that ai​=0 for all but a finite number of indices i. Polynomials are traditionally denoted p(X)=a0​+a1​X+a2​X2+⋯=i=0∑∞​ai​Xi where X is a symbol called indeterminate.

The set of all univariate polynomials over F is denoted F[X]: F[X]:={i=0∑∞​ai​Xi:ai​∈F,∃N∈N,∀i≥N,ai​=0}.

The set F[X] equipped with operations + and ⋅ is a ring called the polynomial ring in X over F. Moreover, if we define scalar multiplication of a polynomial p(X) by a∈F as multiplication by the constant polynomial a(X)=a, then F[X] is a vector space over F. The set {1,X,X2,…} is a basis of this vector space called the monomial basis.

The degree of a polynomial p(X) is the largest power of X occurring in p(X) with a non-zero coefficient, with the convention that polynomial 0 has degree −∞. Hence, p(X)=∑i=0d​ai​Xi has degree d provided ad​=0. We let deg(p) denote the degree of a polynomial p and F(≤d)[X] denote the set of polynomials over F of degree at most d.

Proposition 1. Let p(X) and q(X) be two polynomials in F[X]. Then ​deg(p+q)≤max{deg(p),deg(q)}deg(pq)=deg(p)deg(q).​

Given two non-zero polynomial a(X) and b(X), we say that b(X) divides a(X) or that b(X) is a factor of a(X) if there exists a polynomial q(X) such that a(X)=q(X)b(X). The set F[X] is a Euclidean domain, meaning one can perform euclidean division in F[X]: given two polynomials a(X) and b(X) with b(X)=0, there exists unique polynomials q(X) and r(X) such that a(X)=q(X)b(X)+r(X) and deg(r)<deg(b).

Roots

Given a polynomial p(X)=∑i​ai​Xi in F[X] and an element u∈F, the evaluation of p(X) at u, written p(u), is ∑i​ai​ui. The function from F to F mapping u to p(u) is called the polynomial function associated with p.1 We say that u is a root of p(X) if p(u)=0.

Theorem 1. Let p∈F[X] be a polynomial and u∈F be a field element. Then u is a root of p(X) if and only if X−u divides p(X). More generally, p(u)=v if and only if X−u divides p(X)−v.2

Proof. The if direction is straightforward. For the only if direction, note that since F[X] is Euclidean, there exists polynomials q and r such that p(X)=(X−u)q(X)+r(X) with deg(r)<deg(X−u)=1. Hence, the polynomial r must be a constant. Evaluating the polynomial equality at u, we obtain that r=p(u). Hence, if p(u)=v then p(X)−v=(X−u)q(X), which exactly means that X−u divides p(X)−v.

Theorem 2. Let p∈F[X] be a non-zero polynomial of degree d over F. Then p has at most d distinct roots in F.

Proof. By induction on d. The result clearly holds for d=0. Let d≥1 and assume that the result holds for degree 0,…,d−1. Let p∈F[x] be a polynomial of degree d. If p has no root then the result holds again. Otherwise, assume that p has a root u∈F. Then, by the previous theorem, p(X)=(X−u)q(X) for some polynomial q, where, by Proposition 1, q has degree d−1. If u′ is a root of p distinct from u, then (u′−u)q(u′)=0 which implies that q(u′)=0 since u′−u=0 and F is a field. Since q has at most d−1 distinct roots by the induction hypothesis, p has at most d distinct roots.
