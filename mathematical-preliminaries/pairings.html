Pairings

Pairings have allowed a great number of new applications in cryptography. They were first used by Menezes, Okamoto, and Vanstone [MOV91] to break the discrete logarithm problem in supersingular elliptic curves (this is the so-called MOV attack). Later, they were used constructively by Joux [Jou00] to obtain a tripartite Diffie-Hellman protocol and by Boneh and Franklin [BF03] to obtain an identity-based encryption scheme. This got the three of them the Gödel prize in 2013.

We will start with the abstract definition of pairing groups and then we will see how to construct such groups from specific elliptic curves.

Abstract Definition

Let G1​, G2​, and Gt​ be three cyclic groups of prime order r. For reasons that will become clear later, we will denote G1​ and G2​ additively and Gt​ multiplicatively (in particular, G1​ and G2​'s identity elements are denoted 0 while Gt​'s identity element is denoted 1). A pairing is an efficiently computable function e:G1​×G2​→Gt​ which satisfies two properties:

  • non-degeneracy: P=0 and Q=0 implies e(P,Q)=1;
  • bilinearity: for every P1​,P2​∈G1​ and Q∈G2​ and for every P∈G1​ and Q1​,Q2​∈G2​, e(P1​+P2​,Q)e(P,Q1​+Q2​)​=e(P1​,Q)e(P2​,Q)=e(P,Q1​)e(P,Q2​).​

Note that bilinearity implies the (more useful in practice) property that for every P∈G1​, Q∈G2​, and a,b∈Zr​, e(aP,bQ)=e(P,Q)ab.

Non-degeneracy has many other definitions (all equivalent assuming bilinearity), such as:

  • e is not the constant function 1,
  • if G1​ and G2​ are generators of respectively G1​ and G2​, then e(G1​,G2​) is a generator of Gt​.

Constructing groups admitting a pairing is not very hard. For example, take G1​=G2​=Zr​ (the group of integers mod r equipped with addition), take for Gt​ any cyclic group of order r, let g be a generator of Gt​, and define e(x,y):=gxy.

However, for being useful from a cryptographic point of view, we need the discrete logarithm problem to be hard in the three groups G1​, G2​, and Gt​. (In the example above, while it may be hard in Gt​, it is certainly not in G1​ and G2​.) This is where elliptic curves come to the rescue.

Some more vocabulary: A pairing is said symmetric when G1​=G2​ and asymmetric when G1​=G2​. As we will see, both types can be constructed from elliptic curves. Historically, the first proposed constructions were symmetric, but nowadays the asymmetric type prevails for efficiency reasons. It is an open problem to construct a pairing such that G1​=G2​=Gt​=G and the discrete logarithm problem is (conjectured) hard in G.
